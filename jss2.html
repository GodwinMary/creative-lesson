Elements and Principles of Art/Design

Line, shape, form, colour, texture, balance, contrast, harmony, unity, rhythm, proportion

⬜ +

Learning Objectives

  • - Identify and describe the seven elements of art: line, shape, form, colour, value, texture, and space
  • - Understand and apply the principles of design: balance, contrast, harmony, unity, rhythm, proportion, and emphasis
  • - Analyse how Nigerian artists use these elements and principles in their work
  • - Create artworks that demonstrate effective use of elements and principles

What Are the Elements of Art?

The elements of art are the basic building blocks that artists use to create artwork. Think of them like ingredients in a recipe: every painting, sculpture, or design uses some combination of these elements. There are seven main elements of art.

1. Line

A line is a mark made by a moving point. Lines can be straight, curved, thick, thin, zigzag, or wavy. In Nigerian art, lines are used in uli body painting of the Igbo people and in the geometric patterns of Hausa embroidery (called aska). Lines create movement, outline shapes, and express feelings.

2. Shape

A shape is a flat, enclosed area created by lines. Shapes can be geometric (circles, squares, triangles) or organic (irregular, natural shapes like leaves and clouds). Adire textile patterns use both geometric and organic shapes. The Nsibidi symbols of the Ejagham people are made of combined shapes.

3. Form

Form is the three-dimensional version of shape. While a circle is a shape, a sphere is a form. A square becomes a cube. Nigerian Nok terracotta heads are excellent examples of form: the artists sculpted round, cylindrical, and conical forms to create expressive human faces.

4. Colour

Colour has three properties: hue (the name of the colour), value (lightness or darkness), and intensity (brightness or dullness). The primary colours are red, yellow, and blue. In Nigerian culture, colours carry meaning: white for purity, red for power and danger, green for life and growth.

5. Value

Value refers to the lightness or darkness of a colour. Artists use value to create the illusion of depth and volume in their work. A gradual change from light to dark is called shading. This technique makes flat drawings look three-dimensional and realistic.

6. Texture

Texture is how a surface feels or looks like it would feel. Actual texture is real (like the rough surface of a clay pot), while visual texture is an illusion created by the artist. Nigerian pottery, basketry, and wood carvings are rich in actual texture. Textile arts like aso-oke weaving create distinct textures.

7. Space

Space is the area around, between, and within objects. Positive space is occupied by the main subject, while negative space is the empty area around it. Artists use techniques like overlapping, size variation, and placement to create the illusion of depth. In traditional Nigerian wall paintings, space is used to arrange scenes and tell stories.

What Are the Principles of Design?

The principles of design are the rules or guidelines that artists follow to organise the elements of art into a visually pleasing composition. They tell artists how to arrange the elements effectively.

⚖️

Balance

The distribution of visual weight in a composition. Symmetrical balance means both sides are equal (like a butterfly). Asymmetrical balance uses different elements of equal visual weight on each side. The Benin Bronze plaques demonstrate symmetrical balance, with the Oba (king) placed centrally.

🔲

Contrast

The arrangement of opposite elements (light vs. dark, large vs. small, rough vs. smooth) to create visual interest. Adire cloth uses the contrast between the deep indigo dye and the white resist-dyed areas to create striking patterns.

🎵

Harmony

The combination of similar or related elements to create a pleasing, unified look. Using colours that are close on the colour wheel (like green, blue-green, and blue) creates colour harmony. Nigerian beadwork often uses harmonious colour combinations passed down through generations.

🔗

Unity

The feeling that all parts of an artwork belong together. Unity is achieved when all elements and principles work in harmony. A well-designed Ankara fabric pattern demonstrates unity: the colours, shapes, and patterns all feel like they belong together.

🔄

Rhythm

The repetition of elements to create a sense of visual movement, like the beat in music. Rhythm can be regular (equal spacing), alternating (two elements taking turns), or progressive (gradually changing). The repeated geometric patterns in Hausa architecture show regular rhythm.

📐

Proportion

The size relationship between different parts of an artwork. Proportion can be realistic or exaggerated for effect. In Yoruba twin figures (ere ibeji), the heads are purposely made larger in proportion to the body to emphasise the spiritual importance of the head in Yoruba belief.

👁️

Emphasis

The focal point of an artwork that draws the viewer's attention first. Artists create emphasis through contrast, placement, size, or colour. In the Benin Bronze heads, the elaborate coral bead crown creates emphasis, drawing attention to the royal status of the Oba.

Key Vocabulary

Line Shape Form Colour Value Texture Space Balance Contrast Harmony Unity Rhythm Proportion Emphasis Composition Hue Intensity

Nigerian Traditional Music and Dance Forms

Bata dance, Atilogwu, Swange dance, Koroso, talking drums, cultural significance

⬜ +

Learning Objectives

  • - Identify major Nigerian traditional dance forms and their ethnic origins
  • - Explain the cultural significance and occasions for each dance
  • - Describe the role of traditional instruments, especially the talking drum
  • - Demonstrate basic movements from at least one Nigerian dance form
  • - Appreciate the connection between music, dance, and community life in Nigeria

Music and Dance in Nigerian Culture

In Nigeria, music and dance are not just entertainment. They are essential parts of everyday life, used in worship, celebrations, storytelling, communication, and community bonding. Every ethnic group has its own unique styles of music and dance, passed down from generation to generation. Let us explore some of the most important ones.

Major Nigerian Dance Forms

🪘 Bata Dance (Yoruba)

The Bata dance originates from the Yoruba people of southwestern Nigeria. It is closely connected to the worship of Sango, the Yoruba god of thunder and lightning. The dance is performed to the accompaniment of the bata drum, a double-headed, hourglass-shaped drum that produces deep, resonant sounds.

Bata dancers wear colourful costumes, often in red and white (the colours of Sango). The movements are powerful and energetic, featuring rapid footwork, spinning, and dramatic gestures. The lead dancer responds to the rhythms of the master drummer, creating a conversation between movement and sound.

Occasions: Religious festivals, coronation ceremonies, chieftaincy installations, cultural celebrations.

💃 Atilogwu Dance (Igbo)

The Atilogwu is one of the most acrobatic and exciting dances in Nigeria. The name comes from the Igbo word meaning "is this magic?" because the movements are so skilful that spectators often wonder if the dancers are using magical powers.

Originating from the Afikpo area of present-day Ebonyi State, Atilogwu features incredible feats of agility: high jumps, backflips, rapid spinning, and synchronised group movements. Dancers wear colourful costumes with feathers, beads, and bells on their ankles. The dance is accompanied by drums, flutes (oja), and the ogene (metal gong).

Occasions: New Yam festivals, weddings, cultural competitions, community gatherings.

🎶 Swange Dance (Tiv)

The Swange dance belongs to the Tiv people of Benue State in central Nigeria. It is a relatively modern social dance that emerged in the mid-20th century, blending traditional Tiv dance steps with influences from highlife music. The word "swange" means "to dance" in the Tiv language.

Swange is performed in a circular formation, with dancers moving gracefully in a line. The movements are smooth, flowing, and rhythmic, with gentle hip swaying and coordinated arm movements. Both men and women participate, making it one of the most inclusive Nigerian dances. It is accompanied by drums, flutes, and the thumb piano (adiguve).

Occasions: Social gatherings, moonlight celebrations, festivals, weddings.

🏇 Koroso Dance (Hausa/Fulani)

The Koroso is a traditional dance of the Hausa and Fulani people of northern Nigeria. It is often performed during the famous Durbar festival and other royal occasions. The dance showcases horsemanship, bravery, and the martial traditions of the northern emirates.

Koroso dancers, typically male warriors, perform on horseback or on foot. They wear elaborate costumes including flowing robes (babban riga), turbans, and sometimes carry ceremonial swords and shields. The movements are bold and dignified, reflecting the warrior culture of the Hausa-Fulani. The dance is accompanied by the kalangu (talking drum), kakaki (long trumpet), and ganga (large drum).

Occasions: Durbar festivals, Eid celebrations, royal events, harvest festivals.

The Talking Drum: Nigeria's Speaking Instrument

The talking drum (dundun in Yoruba, kalangu in Hausa) is one of the most remarkable instruments in the world. It can imitate the tones and rhythms of spoken language, effectively allowing the drummer to "speak" through the instrument.

The drum is shaped like an hourglass and has two drumheads connected by leather cords. By squeezing the cords under the arm while striking the drumhead with a curved stick, the drummer can change the pitch and produce sounds that mimic the tonal patterns of Yoruba and other Nigerian languages.

Historically, talking drums were used to:

  • - Send messages across long distances between villages
  • - Announce the arrival of important people
  • - Praise kings and chiefs by reciting their lineage
  • - Accompany dancers and guide their movements
  • - Tell stories and preserve oral history

Today, the talking drum remains a vital instrument in both traditional and contemporary Nigerian music, used by artists like King Sunny Ade and Sikiru Ayinde Barrister.

Other Important Nigerian Instruments

🎺

Kakaki

Long brass trumpet used in Hausa royal courts, up to 2 metres long

🔔

Ogene

Large metal gong used by the Igbo people to set the rhythm for dances

🎵

Oja (Flute)

Igbo wooden flute that produces melodious sounds for dances and ceremonies

Key Vocabulary

Bata Atilogwu Swange Koroso Dundun Kalangu Kakaki Ogene Oja Durbar Sango

Creative Writing and Storytelling

Folktales, proverbs, Nigerian literary traditions, oral tradition

⬜ +

Learning Objectives

  • - Understand the role of oral tradition in Nigerian culture and its elements
  • - Identify different types of Nigerian folktales and their moral lessons
  • - Explain the meaning and use of Nigerian proverbs in storytelling
  • - Write original creative stories inspired by Nigerian literary traditions
  • - Perform storytelling using voice, gestures, and audience engagement

The Power of Nigerian Oral Tradition

Long before books and writing came to Nigeria, stories were passed from one generation to the next by word of mouth. This is called oral tradition. Grandparents told stories to grandchildren around the fire under the moonlight. These stories carried the history, values, wisdom, and beliefs of the people. Today, oral tradition remains one of the most important parts of Nigerian cultural heritage.

Nigerian Folktales

Folktales are traditional stories that have been told and retold over many generations. In Nigerian folktales, animals often behave like humans, speaking, thinking, and making decisions. These are called animal tales or fables. The most famous character in Yoruba folktales is the tortoise (Ijapa), who is clever but often greedy and cunning. In Igbo folktales, the tortoise (Mbe) plays a similar role.

Types of Nigerian Folktales

🐢
Animal Tales (Fables)

Stories where animals act like humans. They teach moral lessons such as the consequences of greed, pride, or dishonesty. Example: "Why the Tortoise Has a Cracked Shell" teaches that greed leads to downfall.

👸
Legends and Hero Tales

Stories about extraordinary people with great abilities. These include tales of warriors, wise kings, and powerful queens such as Queen Amina of Zazzau, Moremi of Ile-Ife, and Oduduwa the founder of the Yoruba people.

🌍
Origin Stories (Myths)

Stories that explain how things came to be: how the world was created, why the sky is high, why certain animals look the way they do. Every ethnic group has its own creation myth.

👻
Dilemma Tales

Stories that end with a difficult question for the audience to discuss and debate. They have no single correct answer and are designed to encourage critical thinking. These are popular across many Nigerian ethnic groups.

Nigerian Proverbs: Wisdom in a Sentence

As the Igbo proverb says, "Proverbs are the palm oil with which words are eaten." Proverbs are short, wise sayings that express a truth or give advice. In Nigerian culture, using proverbs in speech is a sign of wisdom and education. Elders often speak in proverbs, and young people are expected to learn and understand them.

Yoruba Proverbs

  • "Bi a ba n la oruko omo, a n la iwa re." - When we call a child's name, we are calling their character. (Names carry meaning.)
  • "Agba ki wa loja, ki ori omo titun wo." - An elder does not stand in the market and let a child's head go crooked. (Elders must guide the young.)

Igbo Proverbs

  • "Onye aghana nwanne ya." - Let nobody abandon their sibling. (Family unity is paramount.)
  • "E mee nwata ka e mere ibe ya, obi ewe ya." - When a child is treated like their peers, they are happy. (Treat everyone fairly.)

Hausa Proverbs

  • "Ruwan da ke zubar, ba a kwashe shi ba." - Water that has been poured away cannot be collected. (Think before you act.)
  • "Gaskiya ta fi kwabo." - Truth is worth more than a penny. (Honesty is priceless.)

Edo/Benin Proverbs

  • "Ogieva ma gha zuwa, a gha kpolo ne." - Before the rain falls, there is wind. (Every event has a warning sign.)
  • "Oba ghato okpere, ise." - When the king speaks, so it is. (Authority and respect.)

Nigerian Literary Traditions

Nigeria has produced some of the greatest writers in Africa. These authors drew inspiration from oral traditions and folktales to create powerful written literature. Notable Nigerian authors include Chinua Achebe (author of Things Fall Apart, which draws heavily on Igbo proverbs and oral tradition), Wole Soyinka (the first African to win the Nobel Prize for Literature), Amos Tutuola (who wrote The Palm-Wine Drinkard in a style that mirrors Yoruba oral storytelling), and Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie (a contemporary voice in Nigerian literature).

How to Write Your Own Folktale

  1. 1. Choose a lesson or moral: What do you want your reader to learn? (e.g., honesty is the best policy, pride goes before a fall)
  2. 2. Pick your characters: Use animals, humans, or spirits. Give them clear personalities (the clever hare, the greedy tortoise, the wise old woman).
  3. 3. Set the scene: Begin with a traditional opening like "A long, long time ago, in a village far away..." or the Yoruba opening, "Alo o!" (to which the audience responds "Alo!")
  4. 4. Create a problem: The main character must face a challenge or conflict.
  5. 5. Build the story: Show how the character tries to solve the problem. Include dialogue and descriptions.
  6. 6. End with the lesson: Close with the moral and a traditional ending, such as the Yoruba: "And my story ends there; whether it falls into the sea or falls on the ground."
  7. 7. Include proverbs: Weave at least one or two proverbs into your story to add depth and cultural flavour.

Key Vocabulary

Oral tradition Folktale Fable Legend Myth Proverb Moral Narrative Dilemma tale Ijapa Folklore

Sculpture and 3D Art Forms

Modelling, carving, construction, Nigerian sculptural traditions (Nok, Ife, Benin)

⬜ +

Learning Objectives

  • - Define sculpture and distinguish it from two-dimensional art
  • - Identify the three main techniques of sculpture: modelling, carving, and construction
  • - Describe the major Nigerian sculptural traditions: Nok, Ife, and Benin
  • - Explain the materials, tools, and processes used in each technique
  • - Create a simple sculpture using modelling or construction techniques

What Is Sculpture?

Sculpture is the art of creating three-dimensional (3D) forms. Unlike drawings and paintings, which are flat (2D), sculptures have height, width, and depth. You can walk around a sculpture and see it from different angles. Sculptures can be made from many materials including clay, wood, stone, metal, plaster, and even recycled objects.

Three Main Techniques of Sculpture

🏺

Modelling

Modelling is an additive process: you build up the form by adding material. Common modelling materials include clay, plasticine, papier-mache, and plaster of Paris.

Process: The sculptor starts with a lump of soft material and shapes it by adding, pressing, pinching, smoothing, and pulling. Clay can be fired (heated in a kiln) to make it permanent.

Nigerian example: Nok terracotta sculptures were made by modelling clay.

🪵

Carving

Carving is a subtractive process: you remove material from a solid block to reveal the form inside. Materials include wood, stone, ivory, and bone.

Tools: Chisels, mallets, gouges, knives, and files. The sculptor chips away unwanted material piece by piece until the desired shape emerges.

Nigerian example: Yoruba wood carvers create ere ibeji (twin figures), doors, and masks.

🔧

Construction

Construction is an assemblage process: you join separate pieces or materials together to build a sculpture. This includes welding metal, gluing, tying, and fastening.

Materials: Wire, metal scraps, found objects, bamboo, cardboard, and recycled materials. This is the most modern and experimental technique.

Nigerian example: El Anatsui creates massive wall sculptures from recycled bottle caps.

Nigeria's Great Sculptural Traditions

Nigeria has one of the oldest and richest sculptural traditions in the world. Three traditions stand out as masterpieces of human creativity: Nok, Ife, and Benin.

🗿 Nok Terracotta (c. 500 BC - 200 AD)

The Nok culture produced the oldest known sculptures in sub-Saharan Africa. Discovered near the village of Nok in present-day Kaduna State in 1928, these terracotta (fired clay) figures date back over 2,500 years.

Characteristics:

  • - Cylindrical or triangular-shaped heads with elaborate hairstyles
  • - Triangular, pierced eyes with distinct eyebrows
  • - Detailed jewellery and body decorations
  • - Hollow interiors to prevent cracking during firing
  • - Sizes range from small pendants to nearly life-size figures

The Nok people were among the earliest iron smelters in West Africa, and their art represents a remarkably advanced civilisation.

👑 Ife Art (c. 1100 - 1500 AD)

The Ife civilisation in present-day Osun State produced some of the most naturalistic sculptures ever created in Africa. The bronzes and terracottas of Ife are so lifelike that European scholars initially refused to believe Africans had created them.

Characteristics:

  • - Highly realistic and naturalistic representation of the human face
  • - Smooth, idealised features with serene expressions
  • - Fine parallel lines (scarification marks) on the face
  • - Made using the lost-wax casting technique for bronze
  • - Represented Ooni (kings/rulers) and other important figures

The famous bronze head of Queen Olokun is one of the finest pieces, demonstrating the extraordinary skill of Ife artists.

🦁 Benin Bronzes (c. 1400 - 1897 AD)

The Benin Kingdom (in present-day Edo State) produced thousands of magnificent bronze and brass sculptures, plaques, and carvings. These works decorated the royal palace of the Oba and recorded the history and achievements of the kingdom.

Characteristics:

  • - Commemorative heads of Obas with elaborate coral bead crowns and collars
  • - Rectangular plaques depicting court life, warriors, and ceremonies
  • - Animal figures including leopards, roosters, and pythons
  • - Made using the lost-wax casting technique with great technical skill
  • - Highly detailed depictions of clothing, weapons, and regalia

The guild of bronze casters (Igun Eronmwon) in Benin City continues to practice this ancient art today. Many Benin Bronzes were taken during the British punitive expedition of 1897 and are now in museums around the world, with ongoing efforts for their repatriation.

Stage Craft and Drama Production

Set design, costumes, props, lighting basics, directing

⬜ +

Learning Objectives

  • - Define stage craft and explain its importance in drama production
  • - Identify the different parts of a stage and types of stages
  • - Describe the roles of set design, costumes, props, and lighting in a production
  • - Understand the role of the director and the production team
  • - Plan and participate in a simple class drama production

What Is Stage Craft?

Stage craft is the art and technique of creating the physical environment for a theatrical performance. While actors perform on stage, there is a whole team of people working behind the scenes to make the production come alive. Stage craft covers everything the audience sees and hears apart from the actors' performance: the scenery, the lighting, the sound, the costumes, and the props.

Parts of a Stage

Key Stage Areas

  • Centre Stage: The middle of the performing area, the strongest position
  • Downstage: The front of the stage, closest to the audience
  • Upstage: The back of the stage, farthest from the audience
  • Stage Right: The right side from the actor's perspective (facing the audience)
  • Stage Left: The left side from the actor's perspective
  • Wings: The hidden areas on either side of the stage where actors wait

Types of Stages

  • Proscenium Stage: A framed stage with the audience on one side (most common)
  • Thrust Stage: Extends into the audience, with seating on three sides
  • Arena/Theatre-in-the-Round: Audience surrounds the stage on all sides
  • Open-Air Stage: Common in Nigerian traditional performances, performed outdoors in village squares

Elements of Stage Craft

🎨 Set Design (Scenery)

Set design creates the visual environment where the action takes place. The set tells the audience where and when the story is happening: a palace throne room, a market square, a village compound, or a modern city apartment.

Elements include: Backdrops (painted or projected backgrounds), flats (lightweight frames covered with canvas), platforms and levels, furniture, and scenic elements like trees, walls, or doorways. A set designer must consider the script, the director's vision, the available space, and the budget.

👘 Costumes

Costumes tell the audience who each character is without the character having to say a word. They show social status, occupation, personality, historical period, and cultural background.

In Nigerian drama productions, costumes often draw from traditional attire:

  • - An Oba might wear a flowing agbada with a coral bead crown and necklace
  • - A market woman might wear a wrapper (iro) with a blouse (buba) and head tie (gele)
  • - A hunter character could wear a dansiki decorated with charms and animal skins
  • - A masquerade character wears elaborate costumes with carved masks

🪑 Props (Properties)

Props are objects used by actors during a performance. They make the action more realistic and help tell the story. Props are classified into three types:

  • Hand props: Items carried or handled by actors (a staff, a sword, a calabash, a broom, a book)
  • Set props: Larger items that are part of the set (tables, chairs, market stalls, cooking pots)
  • Personal props: Items worn or carried by a specific character throughout the play (a walking stick, a fan, a handbag)

💡 Lighting

Lighting does much more than simply making the stage visible. It creates mood, focuses attention, indicates time of day, and can represent location changes.

Basic lighting techniques:

  • - General wash: Even light across the whole stage
  • - Spotlight: A focused beam on one actor or area, drawing attention
  • - Blackout: All lights go off, used to end scenes or create dramatic moments
  • - Coloured lighting: Red for danger or anger, blue for night or sadness, warm amber for happiness
  • - Fade in/Fade out: Gradually increasing or decreasing light to begin or end a scene

🎬 The Director

The director is the creative leader of the production. Like a captain of a ship, the director guides everyone towards a unified vision for the play. The director's responsibilities include:

  • - Interpreting the script and deciding the overall vision
  • - Casting actors in appropriate roles
  • - Blocking (planning where actors move on stage)
  • - Guiding actors' performances and characterisation
  • - Coordinating with the design team (set, costumes, lighting, sound)
  • - Running rehearsals and ensuring the show is ready by opening night

Nigerian Drama Traditions

Nigeria has a vibrant drama tradition that includes both traditional and modern forms. Traditional Nigerian performances like masquerade festivals, the Alarinjo travelling theatre of the Yoruba, and community storytelling events are all forms of drama that use stage craft elements. Modern Nigerian theatre was pioneered by artists like Hubert Ogunde (the father of Nigerian theatre), Duro Ladipo, Ola Rotimi, and Wole Soyinka, who blended traditional performance styles with Western theatrical techniques.

Cultural Festivals and Celebrations

Eyo festival, Argungu fishing festival, Osun-Osogbo, New Yam festival, Durbar

⬜ +

Learning Objectives

  • - Identify major Nigerian cultural festivals and the ethnic groups that celebrate them
  • - Describe the significance, activities, and arts involved in each festival
  • - Explain how festivals preserve and promote Nigerian cultural heritage
  • - Analyse the role of visual arts, music, dance, and drama in festivals
  • - Appreciate the diversity of Nigerian cultural expressions through festivals

Why Are Festivals Important?

Cultural festivals are special occasions when communities come together to celebrate their heritage, honour their ancestors, give thanks, and strengthen social bonds. Festivals are living museums: they keep traditions alive through music, dance, costumes, art, food, and rituals. Nigeria, with its rich diversity of over 250 ethnic groups, has hundreds of colourful festivals throughout the year. Let us explore some of the most important ones.

Eyo Festival (Adamu Orisa Play)

Lagos - Yoruba People

The Eyo Festival is one of the most spectacular festivals in Lagos. It is performed to honour the memory of a deceased prominent Lagos chief or Oba. The festival features the Eyo masquerades: tall figures dressed entirely in white flowing robes and wearing wide-brimmed hats called agogoro.

The Eyo masquerades dance through the streets of Lagos Island, accompanied by drums and chanting. There are different groups of Eyo, each representing a different family or district. The festival brings all of Lagos together and is sometimes called "the forerunner of the modern carnival."

Arts involved: Costume design (white robes and hats), dance, drumming, masquerade performance, procession choreography.

Argungu International Fishing Festival

Kebbi State - Hausa/Kebbawa People

The Argungu Fishing Festival is held annually along the banks of the Matan Fada River in Argungu, Kebbi State. It began in 1934 to mark the end of centuries of hostility between the Sokoto Caliphate and the Kebbi Kingdom.

The main event is a dramatic fishing competition where thousands of fishermen, armed only with hand nets and calabash gourds, jump into the river at the sound of a gunshot and compete to catch the biggest fish within one hour. The festival also features swimming competitions, canoe races, bare-hand fishing, diving competitions, wild duck catching, and a grand durbar parade.

Arts involved: Net weaving and calabash decoration, traditional drumming, Koroso dance, elaborate traditional clothing, pottery exhibitions.

Osun-Osogbo Festival

Osun State - Yoruba People

The Osun-Osogbo Festival is a two-week celebration held annually in August at the Osun-Osogbo Sacred Grove in Osogbo, Osun State. The grove is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, one of the last remaining sacred groves in Yoruba culture, and contains shrines, sculptures, and art works honouring the river goddess Osun.

The festival culminates with the Arugba (the sacred calabash carrier), a young maiden chosen to carry a covered calabash containing offerings to the Osun River. The procession follows her from the palace of the Ataoja (king) of Osogbo to the sacred grove. The festival attracts thousands of worshippers, tourists, and cultural enthusiasts from around the world.

Arts involved: Sculpture (the grove contains remarkable artworks by Susanne Wenger and local artists), traditional Yoruba music and drumming, dance, body painting, elaborate priestess costumes, ritual performances.

New Yam Festival (Iri Ji / Iwa Ji)

Southeast Nigeria - Igbo People

The New Yam Festival (Iri Ji Ohuru or Iwa Ji) is the most important festival of the Igbo people, celebrating the end of the farming season and the harvesting of the new yam. Yam is considered the "king of crops" in Igbo culture and symbolises prosperity, hard work, and the blessings of the earth.

The festival typically takes place between August and October, depending on the community. The oldest man in the village offers the first yam to the gods and ancestors, giving thanks for a bountiful harvest. After this, everyone is free to eat the new yam. The celebration includes feasting, masquerade displays, wrestling competitions, the Atilogwu dance, and the presentation of the biggest yam by competing farmers.

Arts involved: Masquerade carving and costume making, Atilogwu dance, drumming and flute music, body decoration, calabash carving and painting, food presentation.

Durbar Festival

Northern Nigeria - Hausa/Fulani People

The Durbar is a grand royal festival celebrated in cities like Kano, Katsina, Zaria, and Bauchi to mark the end of Eid-el-Fitr (to celebrate the end of Ramadan) and Eid-el-Kabir (the festival of sacrifice). The word "Durbar" originally comes from the Urdu/Hindi language and refers to a royal assembly or court.

The festival is a dazzling display of horsemanship, military tradition, and royal splendour. Hundreds of horsemen dressed in magnificent traditional regalia ride to the Emir's palace to pay homage. The horses are adorned with colourful quilted armour, silver ornaments, and bells. The procession includes musicians playing the kakaki (long trumpet), kalangu (talking drum), and ganga (large drum). The highlight is the jahi: a cavalry charge where riders gallop at full speed towards the Emir, stopping dramatically just before him to salute.

Arts involved: Textile art (elaborate embroidered robes and turbans), metalwork (horse ornaments and weapons), leatherwork (horse saddles and armour), drumming and trumpet playing, Koroso dance, equestrian displays.

How Festivals Preserve Culture

Festivals serve several vital purposes in preserving Nigerian cultural heritage:

1.

Transmission: They pass traditions, skills, and values from elders to young people through participation and observation.

2.

Unity: They bring communities together, strengthening social bonds and shared identity.

3.

Arts promotion: They keep traditional arts alive: drumming, dance, costume-making, sculpture, and crafts.

4.

Tourism: They attract visitors from around the world, generating income and international recognition for Nigerian culture.
